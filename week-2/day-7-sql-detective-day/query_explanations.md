# Query Explanations — SQL Detective Day (Day 7)

This document provides a breakdown of the database queries used to extract and verify our weekly business insights. Each explanation covers what the query does, the tables involved, why a JOIN was or wasn't necessary, and how to interpret the results.

---

## Part 1: Explanations of Fixed Queries (8 Selected)

### 1. Fixed Query 1: Order Count by City
* **What it does:** This query displays a list of cities alongside the total number of orders placed by customers living in each city.
* **Tables used:** `orders` and `customers`
* **Why JOIN is needed:** The `orders` table contains the transaction records, but it does not store customer location details. We need to JOIN with the `customers` table using the shared `customer_id` column to match each order to the customer's city.
* **What the result means:** It identifies where our order volume is physically concentrated. For example, seeing that Prishtina has 5 orders and Peja has 1 helps us understand where the bulk of our local shipping and marketing resources should be focused.

### 2. Fixed Query 2: Completed Revenue by Product
* **What it does:** It calculates the total money generated by each specific product name, strictly filtering for orders that successfully completed.
* **Tables used:** `orders` and `products`
* **Why JOIN is needed:** The `orders` table records how many items were purchased (`quantity`) and the order's status, but it doesn't store the product names or their individual costs. We JOIN with the `products` table on `product_id` to match the quantity to the product's unit price and product name.
* **What the result means:** This helps managers identify which physical products are driving real cash flow. Knowing that the Laptop brought in $700 while the Mouse brought in $105 helps guide inventory decisions, showing us which items are high-revenue and worth keeping in stock.

### 3. Fixed Query 3: Order Count by Status
* **What it does:** It counts how many orders fall into each status category (completed, pending, or cancelled) and lists them from most to least frequent.
* **Tables used:** `orders`
* **Why JOIN is NOT needed:** All the information required to run this query — specifically the order rows and the `status` column — is stored directly in the `orders` table. There is no need to fetch names, prices, or locations from other tables.
* **What the result means:** It gives a quick health check of the ordering pipeline. If cancelled or pending orders are higher than completed ones, it signals operational issues or friction in the checkout process.

### 4. Fixed Query 4: Total Amount per Order Line
* **What it does:** It lists individual orders and multiplies the quantities purchased by the product prices to show the total transaction amount for each order.
* **Tables used:** `orders` and `products`
* **Why JOIN is needed:** While `orders` has the transaction ID and quantity, it lacks the actual pricing information. We JOIN with the `products` table on `product_id` so we can multiply `quantity` from `orders` by `price` from `products` to calculate the final transaction line.
* **What the result means:** This provides a detailed breakdown of individual transaction values, allowing us to see which specific orders were high-ticket purchases.

### 5. Fixed Query 5: Completed Order Quantities by Product Category
* **What it does:** It summarizes the total physical units sold for each product category (such as Electronics or Accessories), looking only at completed sales.
* **Tables used:** `orders` and `products`
* **Why JOIN is needed:** The `orders` table records the quantities sold, but the category classification (e.g., Electronics, Accessories) lives in the `products` table. We JOIN the two tables on `product_id` to correctly group the transaction quantities under their respective categories.
* **What the result means:** It shows product volume trends at a department level. For example, it tells us if we are selling more units of Accessories overall, even if Electronics is generating more money.

### 6. Fixed Query 7: Customers with Multiple Orders
* **What it does:** It filters the customer base to show only the customer IDs of individuals who placed more than one order during the week.
* **Tables used:** `orders`
* **Why JOIN is NOT needed:** Each order record in the `orders` table already contains a `customer_id`. Since we only need the ID and a count of how many times it appears, we can group the orders by `customer_id` and filter using `HAVING COUNT(*) > 1` without touching the customer profile details.
* **What the result means:** It identifies repeat customers. These customers are highly valuable for loyalty programs and represent a stable user base that likes the service enough to return.

### 7. Fixed Query 8: Order ID and Customer Name Match
* **What it does:** It returns a simple lookup list linking every single order ID to the name of the customer who placed it.
* **Tables used:** `orders` and `customers`
* **Why JOIN is needed:** The transaction records in `orders` only list customer IDs, not names. To make the report readable for humans, we JOIN with the `customers` table on `customer_id` to retrieve the actual name (e.g., "Arta" instead of "1").
* **What the result means:** It is a basic auditing list that lets support or sales teams quickly see exactly who is behind a specific transaction ID.

### 8. Fixed Query 10: Non-Completed Orders
* **What it does:** It displays all fields for orders that do not have a status of 'completed' (i.e., those that are pending or cancelled).
* **Tables used:** `orders`
* **Why JOIN is NOT needed:** All order metadata, including dates, quantities, and their current transaction status, resides directly inside the `orders` table.
* **What the result means:** This acts as a worklist of issues or potential losses. Pending orders represent money waiting to be claimed, while cancelled orders represent lost revenue that needs to be investigated.

## Part 2: Explanations of Validation Queries (5 Selected)

### 1. Validation Query 1: Total Order Count
* **What it does:** It counts the absolute total number of rows in the orders database to find the total volume of orders placed.
* **Tables used:** `orders`
* **Why JOIN is NOT needed:** The query only counts rows in a single table, so there is no reason to pull relationships or columns from `products` or `customers`.
* **What the result means:** It establishes the baseline metric for overall activity (14 orders). All other status-based metrics are subsets of this total count.

### 2. Validation Query 7: Total Completed Revenue
* **What it does:** It sums up the total dollar value of all completed sales, ignoring any cancelled or pending transactions.
* **Tables used:** `orders` and `products`
* **Why JOIN is needed:** The actual unit price lives in the `products` table, while the quantity and status live in the `orders` table. A JOIN on `product_id` is required to perform the multiplication (`quantity * price`) for each completed transaction and add them together.
* **What the result means:** This is the primary financial metric for the business. It shows the actual cash that came in during the week ($1,639).

### 3. Validation Query 9: Completed Revenue by Category
* **What it does:** It calculates the total money earned in each product category, considering only orders with a status of 'completed'.
* **Tables used:** `orders` and `products`
* **Why JOIN is needed:** We need the product price (to calculate revenue) and the category classification (to group the results), both of which are in the `products` table, alongside the order quantity and status from the `orders` table.
* **What the result means:** It highlights which parts of the catalog are the major financial drivers. Seeing that Electronics brought in $1,240 tells us that the business depends heavily on high-value electronics.

### 4. Validation Query 11: Repeat Customer Activity
* **What it does:** It identifies customer IDs that have placed more than one order, acting as a validation step for user engagement.
* **Tables used:** `orders`
* **Why JOIN is NOT needed:** We are grouping and filtering using columns (`customer_id`) that are already present on each row of the `orders` table.
* **What the result means:** It confirms the presence of repeat buyers in our system (4 customers had exactly 2 orders each), verifying that we have a healthy level of weekly customer retention.

### 5. Validation Query 13: Orders Excluded from Revenue
* **What it does:** It lists all orders that were pending or cancelled, along with their potential dollar value, to show what was excluded from real revenue.
* **Tables used:** `orders` and `products`
* **Why JOIN is needed:** We need to calculate the potential value of these orders by joining `orders` with `products` on `product_id` to get the item price.
* **What the result means:** It shows the total potential revenue that did not complete ($1,660). This highlights how much money is sitting in limbo (pending) or lost (cancelled), giving teams clear targets to follow up on.
